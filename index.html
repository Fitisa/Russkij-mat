Ruskij mat

/Русский мат/

Anna Fitiskina

Oxford, Trinity College, June 2025

For the personal use of Jingyuan Deng

The corpus of Russian obscene vocabulary encompasses hundreds of words, and any native speaker may, on a daily basis, invent new expressions by drawing upon the rich morphological resources of the Russian language.

From my perspective as a native speaker, genuine mat (obscene language) consists of only three fundamental roots and the multitude of words derived from them.

These are:

xuj, noun ‘a penis’

p’izdá, noun ‘a vulva’

jibát’, verb ‘to copulate’

Apart from these, there are two considerably less vulgar obscene words without which it is impossible to speak proper Russian mat.

These are as follows:

bl’at’ noun ‘a prostitute’

súka noun ‘a bitch’ (a female dog)

A crucial caveat. In Russian, mat is significantly more expressive than its English counterparts. It is employed to insult, to intensify speech with emotional and stylistic colour, or to relieve psychological stress. All examples in this glossary are either directed at men or used by men. When directed at women, mat becomes even more intense and offensive, and such usage is beyond the scope of this discussion. The use of mat in polite society — particularly in the presence of elders, children, or women — is regarded as a serious breach of decorum.

Another important note. While the original meanings of these roots — especially the first three — are linked to the so-called ‘lower bodily functions’, in actual usage within obscenities they are fully desemanticised. That is, their meaning is generated almost exclusively through morphological modifications (prefixes and suffixes), with the root functioning as an empty variable.

A rich repertoire of euphemisms

As previously noted, Russian mat is characterised by a high degree of expressivity. Consequently, native speakers often exhibit a tendency to attenuate its impact through the use of euphemisms. For example, the principal euphemisms for the word xuj include: xjer – originally the name of the Cyrillic letter corresponding to the initial of xuj, now commonly used with the same literal meaning; xr’en – the Russian word for horseradish, and fik – referring to a specific obscene hand gesture in which the thumb is thrust between the index and middle fingers.

Let us illustrate the richness of euphemistic expression by examining the phrase xuj s taboj. Its various euphemised forms differ subtly in tone and meaning. For instance, the final variant may no longer qualify as obscene at all, instead conveying a sense of mild frustration mixed with a certain degree of affection.

  • xuj s taboj
  • x’er s taboj
  • xr’en s taboj
  • fik s taboj
  • chort s taboj – ‘the devil with you’
  • p’os s taboj – ‘a dog with you’
  • shut s taboj – ‘a jocker with you’
  • Boh s taboj – ‘God with you’

Practical skills

At times, individuals may engage in speech composed almost entirely of mat. This can sound exceedingly harsh, yet in some contexts, it may attain the status of a distinct form of linguistic artistry. Many of the expressions presented above can be combined to produce novel and highly idiosyncratic meanings.
